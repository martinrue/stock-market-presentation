What Is Trading?

Buying

Buy something now because you think it will appreciate in value

And Then Selling

And sell it later at a higher price – the difference in price is profit

Example: Going Long

Buy

iPhones are going to appreciate, so we buy 10 at £500 each for a total investment of £5,000

Sell

And we're right – they appreciate to £550
£550 x 10 - £5000 leaves a total profit of £500

Nothing Groundbreaking

Price Goes Up = Make Money

So far this probably fits with your mental model of the stock market. People buy shares, keep them for 30 years and make money if the price has risen

On The Other Hand

What happens when the price falls?

When Prices Fall

Quick, Panic

Many assume that when the market is going down people are losing money

Not Necessarily

Only those who are long (buying) are losing, but you can trade short (selling) too

Example: Going Short

Sell

This time, iPhones are going to depreciate, so we want to sell 10 of them

But

We don't actually have 10 iPhones, so we borrow them from people that do (for a fee) and sell them at their current price of £500

Example: Going Short

We're Right Again

The price of an iPhone falls to £450

Buy Back

We have £5000 and a debt of 10 iPhones, which we buy back at their new price of £450 leaving a profit of £500 - fee (£5000 - 10 x £450)

Bull & Bear Markets

Money Can Be Made In Both

Because you can buy and short sell, you can be on either side of a market movement

Case In Point

On Black Wednesday, George Soros shorted GBP knowing it would be devalued and made over US $1 billion

What Can Be Traded?

  • Metals such as gold, silver and copper
  • Commodities such as oil, cotton and sugar
  • Stocks such as APPL, MSFT and IBM
  • Bonds, which are government-backed securities
  • Currency, which is traded in pairs such as EUR/USD (number of euros per dollar)

Why Trade?

To Make Money

If you're good at it you can make a lot of money

Hedging

But the stock market can be used for other purposes such as hedging

Hedging

What Is It?

Hedging allows us to protect ourselves from variable elements of a future contract

How It Works

You take a position in the market that becomes profitable if your contract becomes more expensive and unprofitable if your contract becomes cheaper

Example: Hedging

The Future Contract

We've just entered into a contract to buy a machine from Germany for 100,000 euros, but it won't be ready for 6 months

The Cost Today

EUR/GBP is currently 0.88, meaning our German machine will cost £88,000 today

Example: Hedging

The Problem

If the euro strengthens (insert laughter here), our German machine will cost us more money – if it moved to 0.98, the same machine would cost us £10,000 more

The Solution

We buy EUR/GBP at the current price of 0.88 for 100,000 euros

Example: Hedging

Now The Euro Can Go Up

If the euro appreciates to 0.98, the machine will cost £98,000 but we'll have made £10,000 on the EUR/GBP trade to account for it

Or Down

If the euro falls to 0.78, our trade will be losing £10,000 but the machine will now cost £78,000 – the total cost in both situations is £88,000

Types of Trading

Fundamental Trading

Trading based on a fundamental understanding of the underlying market and predicting why the market will move

Technical Trading

Trading based on the current price and technical indicators only (mainly using charts)

Hybrid

Using a mix of both

How

Get A Broker

A broker is responsible for executing your trades, some also give you charting tools

Different Brokers

  • DMA brokers who execute your trades on the real underlying market with other traders
  • Market Makers who set their own prices (based on the underlying market) – this spread betting in the UK

DMA Brokers

Charge Commission

The DMA broker charges commission for each trade and those trades happen, ultimately, on real exchanges

Tax

Because real underlying stocks/commodities/bonds are traded, earnings are taxable

Better Prices

Actual market rates mean that you'll get the best price available

Market Makers

No Commission

Market makers don't charge commission but they set prices, which includes a 'spread'

No Tax

Because no real underlying stocks/commodities/bonds are traded, it's not taxable

Spread Betting

In the UK the most common type of market maker is a spread betting company

Market Psychology

Herding Effect

Traders often act together much like a herd of animals

The Self Fulfilling Prophecy

When the price is falling for example, people want to get in on the action and sell too, causing it to fall further

Summary

  • Money can be made in up and down markets (you can go long or short)
  • You can trade various commodities – choose one and learn how that thing moves
  • You need a broker to execute your trades
  • Find a strategy to trade based on fundamental, technical or other (migrating elephants) factors
